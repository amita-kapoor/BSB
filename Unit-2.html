Unit 2: The Data Detective#

Learning Outcomes#

By the end of this unit, students will be capable of

  • Recognizing rows and columns in a dataset stored as a DataFrame

  • Explaining the meaning of mean, median, and mode

  • Calculating basic statistical measures using Python

  • Interpreting patterns in data using tables and histograms

Introduction#

Have you ever watched a detective in a story? They don‚Äôt guess. They observe, compare, collect clues, and finally discover the hidden pattern.

A Data Detective does exactly the same thing, but instead of fingerprints and footprints, we look at numbers inside a dataset.

A story from the past#

Long before computers existed, Indian scholars were already practicing the skills of a Data Detective - observing nature carefully, noting patterns, and drawing reasoned conclusions. Their tools were simple: the sky, the seasons, and years of patient observation.

In ancient India, astronomers and scholars followed the principle of Parƒ´k·π£ƒÅ - systematic observation. Texts such as the BrÃ•hat Sa·πÅhitƒÅ (6th century CE) describe how scholars watched cloud formations, winds, rainfall patterns, and the movement of the sun and stars. Their goal was not guesswork, but careful measurement and logical inference, known as AnumƒÅna.

These observations were recorded over long periods, sometimes in palm-leaf manuscripts, and later used to understand:

  • When the monsoon might arrive

  • How seasonal temperature varied

  • Which months were favourable for farming

  • How celestial patterns relate to timekeeping

While they did not have digital datasets, they did something very similar: They collected data, looked for patterns, and made predictions based on evidence.

In that sense, when you analyse weather data today, you are continuing a tradition that goes back more than a thousand years ‚Äî only with tools that scholars of the past would have considered magical.

üìò Learning Note

In Python, a dictionary is a way of storing information in pairs. Each pair has a name and a list of values. You can think of it like a labelled box, where each label has its own list. For example, in our weather data:

  • ‚ÄúTemperature‚Äù is a label, and it has a list of temperature values

  • ‚ÄúHumidity‚Äù is another label, with its own list

  • ‚ÄúAirPressure‚Äù is another label, with its own list All these lists have the same length, so each position together forms one row of data. When we give this dictionary to pandas, it converts it into a DataFrame. A DataFrame is simply a digital table, like a table you draw in your notebook, with:

  • column names at the top

  • rows of values below

So in simple terms:

  • Dictionary helps us organise data by column

  • DataFrame helps us see and work with the data as a table This is why we first write the data as a dictionary and then convert it into a DataFrame.

Activity 2.2: Becoming a Statistical Detective#

Now that your dataset is ready, you can begin examining it just like a detective examines clues. One of the first ways to understand a list of numbers is to look at what is ‚Äútypical‚Äù or ‚Äúcentral.‚Äù You have already learned three useful ideas: the mean, the median, and the mode. Python can calculate these for you using simple commands.

We will begin with the Temperature column. The DataFrame stores this column under the name ‚ÄúTemperature‚Äù, so we can ask Python to look at it and compute these values.

mean_temp = df["Temperature"].mean()
median_temp = df["Temperature"].median()
mode_temp = df["Temperature"].mode()[0]

print(f"Mean Temperature: {mean_temp} \nMedian Temperature: {median_temp} \nMode Temperature: {mode_temp}")

Mean Temperature: 24.4 
Median Temperature: 24.0 
Mode Temperature: 22

When you run this, Python will give you three numbers. These help you understand the overall behaviour of the temperature across the ten days. The mean tells you the average, the median tells you the middle value when the temperatures are arranged in order, and the mode tells you which temperature occurred most often.

Try doing the same exercise manually and compare the time it took you to calculate these numbers versus the computer.#

You can do the same for the Humidity column. This lets you compare the two measures and get a sense of whether humidity varies more or less than temperature in this small dataset.

mean_hum = df["Humidity"].mean()
median_hum = df["Humidity"].median()
mode_hum = df["Humidity"].mode()[0]

print(f"Mean Humidity: {mean_hum} \nMedian Humidity: {median_hum} \nMode Humidity: {mode_hum}")

Mean Humidity: 63.9 
Median Humidity: 63.0 
Mode Humidity: 58

As you look at the results, notice how these numbers act like summaries of the whole column. Instead of examining all ten values one by one, these three measures help you understand the overall pattern at a glance. This is exactly what a Data Detective does‚Äîuse a few smart calculations to make sense of many values.

If you want to compare columns directly, you can print the results together:

print("Temperature:", mean_temp, median_temp, mode_temp)
print("Humidity:   ", mean_hum, median_hum, mode_hum)

Activity 2.3: Visualising Humidity with a Histogram#

Numbers become much easier to understand when you can see them. In the same way that ancient scholars watched cloud shapes or star positions to notice patterns, you can look at a picture of your data to understand how values are spread out. One of the simplest and most powerful ways to do this is with a histogram.

A histogram shows how often certain ranges of values appear. If you think of the humidity values as grains collected in different pots, a histogram tells you how full each pot is. Higher bars mean those values occurred more frequently; lower bars mean they were less common.

Python can draw this picture for you using the plotting library matplotlib. We will create a histogram of the Humidity column.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

plt.hist(df["Humidity"], bins=5, edgecolor="black")
plt.xlabel("Humidity (%)")
plt.ylabel("Frequency")
plt.title("Humidity Distribution")
plt.show()

plt.hist(df["Humidity"], bins=10, edgecolor="black")
plt.show()

More bins divide the humidity values into smaller ranges, giving you a more detailed picture. Fewer bins give you a simpler overview. A Data Detective tries both to get a clear understanding of the data.

In the next activity, you will continue this detective work by interpreting the patterns you see and connecting them to the idea of making simple inferences from data.

Activity 2.4: Interpreting the Patterns You See#

Now that you have drawn a histogram, you can begin to interpret it the way a detective interprets clues. A histogram is more than a picture; it is a summary of many values at once. Instead of reading every humidity number, you can look at the bars and understand what the data is trying to tell you.

Start by looking at which bars are the tallest. Taller bars mean that the humidity values in that range occurred more often. Shorter bars tell you that those values were less common. This simple observation already tells you something about the overall pattern of humidity for the ten days.

To help you reflect as a Data Detective, think about these simple questions:

  • Do most days fall into a single humidity range, or are they spread across different ranges?

  • Are there any humidity values that seem unusual or far from the others?

  • If you had to describe the humidity for these ten days to someone who did not see the table or the plot, what would you say?

You can also compare the histogram with the statistical measures you calculated earlier. For example, if the mean and median humidity are close together, and the histogram looks fairly balanced on both sides, that tells you the humidity values are not too skewed or uneven.

Sometimes it helps to print the mean and median again to keep them in mind while you look at the histogram:

print("Mean Humidity:", mean_hum)
print("Median Humidity:", median_hum)

Mean Humidity: 63.9
Median Humidity: 63.0

The goal of this activity is not to get a single correct answer but to practice the habit of observing, comparing, and drawing simple conclusions. This is the same habit ancient scholars used when they studied patterns in the sky and weather. By combining numbers with visual patterns, you get a clearer picture of what the dataset is telling you.

In the next part, you will wrap up your detective work by creating a short summary of your findings. This small reflection will help you strengthen your understanding of how to read data and explain what you discover.

Activity 2.5: Your Data Detective Summary#

You have now explored the dataset in three different ways: by reading the numbers, by calculating the mean‚Äìmedian‚Äìmode, and by looking at a histogram. Each method gives you a different kind of clue. The final step is to put these clues together and write a short summary of what you discovered.

Look at your DataFrame again, your statistical measures, and your histogram, and try to describe what the data is showing. A summary does not need to be long. It just needs to explain what patterns you noticed.

Here are some guiding questions you can think about while writing:

  • What can you say about the temperature values over these ten days? Hint: Look at how the temperatures move from day to day. Are they jumping a lot or slowly increasing? Do they stay in a narrow range or a wide range?

  • Does the humidity seem mostly high, mostly low, or somewhere in the middle? Hint: Think about the numbers in the Humidity column and the bars in your histogram. Which humidity range had the tallest bar? That tells you where most of the days are.

  • Did the histogram show a clear cluster or spread-out values? Hint: Look at whether many bars are tall in one area, or if the bars are more evenly spread out across the whole x-axis. A cluster means many values gathered close together.

  • Which measure (mean, median, or mode) helped you the most in understanding the data? Hint: Think about which value felt closest to what you saw in the table and the histogram. Did the mean seem typical? Did the median match the middle? Did the mode appear often in the list?

A Data Detective always pauses at the end to make sense of the clues. This habit of reflecting - of asking ‚Äúwhat does this data really tell me?‚Äù - is the beginning of real analysis.
